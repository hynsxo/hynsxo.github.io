About

While tracking down why a drum-playing robot's arm kept drifting slightly out of sync, I ended up re-reading the communication protocol specification from page one. That same investigation led me to take a physics course I needed along the way — and finish it with top marks.

Looking back, every project I've worked on circles the same question: where is this data coming from, where is it going, and how accurately is it getting there? Whether it was CAN communication in a robot, noise data along a UAM flight path, an overseas-remittance pipeline at a bank, or V2V communication between vehicles — only the domain changed. The way I checked the answer never did.

When familiar coursework isn't enough to solve a problem, I go wherever the answer is — a physics class, a protocol spec, whatever it takes. The projects below are a record of the choices I made along the way, and what I learned from them.

🤖 Robotics / EmbeddedDec 2024 – Feb 2025
Undergraduate Field Intern, AI Research Center
Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST) — 10-DOF Dual-Arm Drum-Playing Robot
C/C++CAN (CANopen)GDB

Tracing the robot arm's unnatural motion all the way down, I found the real problem wasn't the hardware — it was communication latency during control-mode switching. Redesigning that layer around a hybrid protocol cut the development timeline by roughly two months.

  • Confirmed that motor position-value synchronization lagged every time the system switched between position and torque control modes, and judged that the existing PDO-only scheme couldn't deliver the needed real-time performance.
  • Proposed and implemented a hybrid PDO+SDO communication scheme to read real-time motor position values directly.
  • Built a trajectory-generation algorithm (Dijkstra + interpolation) to minimize unnecessary torso movement, tuned motor PID parameters, and debugged system-level issues with GDB.
  • Set up a GitHub-convention-based version-control workflow to support future contributors.

Result: Natural drum-strike motion, documented as a performance-state diagram for future contributors — and the project that taught me to go back to the fundamentals when the familiar approach doesn't work.
